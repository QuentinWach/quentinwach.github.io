# Lecture 1
## Introduction
**Statistical mechanics is not really modern physics. It's premodern physics, it's modern physics, and I assure you it will be postmodern physics. The second law of thermodynamics will probably outlast anything.** Times and times again, the second law of thermodynamics has been a guide light to know what we are talking about and to make sure we make sense. Thermodynamics and statistical mechanics may not be as sexy as the Higgs boson but I assure you it is at least as deep. _(It is a lot deeper. My particle physicist friends shouldn't disown me.)_ ... It's a lot deeper. It's a lot more general. And it covers a lot more ground in explaining the world as we know it. And in fact, without statistical mechanics, we would probably know a lot less about the Higgs boson. So for a starting point: What is statistical mechanics about? 

Well, let's take a step back. The basics laws of physics, Netwon's laws, the principles of classical physics, quantum mechanics, and so forth... those things are all about predictability, perfect predictability. Now you might say you can't predict things perfectly in quantum mechanics and that is true but there are some things you can predict perfectly and those things are the predictables of quantum mechanics. Again, as in classical mechanics, you can make your predictions with maximum precision if you know the starting point, which we call the initial conditions, and the laws of the evolution of the system.

If you can measure, or you know for whatever reason, the starting point of the closed system - a closed system means one which is either everything or it is sufficiently isolated from everything else that the other things in the system don't influence it ... if you have a closed system, if you know the initial conditions **exactly** or at least with a whatever precision is necessary and you know the laws of evolution of the system, you have complete predictability and that is all there is to say. Now of course in many cases complete predictability would be completely useless. Having a list of all the particles, their positions and velocities would not be very useful to us. The list would be too long. And subject to rather quick changes as a matter of fact. You you might say that while the basics laws of physics are very very powerful in their predictability, they can also be, in many cases, totally useless for actually analyzing what is going on.

Statistical mechanics as applied to physics systems is basically just probability theory. But when is it applicable? When you don't know the initial conditions with perfect precision. It is applicable if you don't know the laws of motion with infinite precision. And it is applicable when the system is not closed! Then what do you resort to? You resort to probabilities. But because the number of molecules in this room is so large and probabilities tend to become very very precise predictors when the laws of large numbers are applicable, statistical mechanics itself can be highly predictable. But not for everything!

As an illustration: You have a box of gas. It might even be an isolated box of gas.
